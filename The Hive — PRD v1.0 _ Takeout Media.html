01What Is The Hive?

The Hive is a custom-built web platform that runs Takeout Media's operations from end to end. Think of it as two things working together: a back-office dashboard for the Takeout team to manage clients, track accounts, and organize content — and a client-facing portal where brands can log in, see what's being worked on for them, and give approvals.

Right now, most agencies juggle client approvals over WhatsApp, revisions over email, and content scheduling in a separate tool. The Hive puts everything in one place — so nothing slips through the cracks and clients always feel in the loop.

The simple version: Clients sign up, pick a plan, connect their social accounts, and review content Takeout uploads for them. Takeout's team sees everything from their dashboard — who's onboarded, what's pending, what needs attention — and they communicate and deliver through the same platform.
02What Already Exists (and What The Hive Does Differently)

Several tools in the market already solve parts of this problem. Looking at them shows both what works and where the gaps are — gaps that The Hive is specifically designed to fill for Takeout Media's workflow.

Planable
Closest match
Built for agencies managing client content approvals. Teams create posts, clients get a clean view to approve or comment. Very visual and easy for clients to use.
Missing: client onboarding, plan selection, billing, or a Takeout-team dashboard. It's a content tool, not a full CRM + operations platform.
Sendible
Agency-focused
Social scheduling platform built for agencies managing multiple clients. Supports white-label client reports and team workflows.
Missing: built-in client onboarding flow, package selection, or a clear sales-to-delivery pipeline for the agency itself.
SocialPilot
Affordable multi-brand
Manages multiple client accounts and social profiles from one place. Good for scheduling and analytics at a reasonable price point.
Missing: client-side portal where clients can self-onboard, pick plans, and manage their team. It's an internal tool only.
CoSchedule
Content calendar
Centralizes content planning, scheduling, and task management into a single calendar. Strong project management angle for content teams.
Missing: a client portal, client onboarding, or anything that lets the end client interact with the work being done for them.
What The Hive adds: A complete pipeline — from a new client discovering Takeout Media, picking a package, connecting their accounts, all the way to seeing their published content and analytics. It's Planable's approval experience + a CRM for the agency + a proper client onboarding flow, all in one.

05The Client Journey — Step by Step

Here's what a client experiences from the moment they find Takeout Media to the moment they're seeing published results. Note that while clients can do every step themselves, the Account Manager can also complete any of these steps on their behalf — useful when a client needs extra support getting set up.

What the client does
1
Signs up and creates an account
The client lands on The Hive and registers with their email. They fill in basic info about their business. This is the entry point — clean, fast, and welcoming.
2
Picks a plan that fits their budget and goals
They choose from Takeout Media's service packages — each showing clearly what's included (number of posts, platforms, reports, etc.) and the price. They pay or start a trial.
3
Onboards their brand
They fill in their brand profile — name, logo, brand colours, tone of voice, target audience, and what they want to achieve. This brief feeds directly into the Takeout team's workflow. If the client needs help, the Account Manager can fill this in on their behalf.
4
Connects their social media accounts
They log in with Instagram, Facebook, X, or LinkedIn to connect the accounts they want Takeout to manage. The platform stores these securely — no sharing passwords, it uses official login (OAuth). The Account Manager can also do this connection on the client's behalf if needed.
5
Adds their team (optional)
If they have a marketing manager or comms team, they can invite them in as viewers. The Client Admin keeps full control, but the team can see what's happening and leave comments.
6
Receives content for review
When the Takeout team uploads content, the client gets notified. They log in, see the post exactly as it will look on the platform, and can approve, request changes, or leave a comment.
7
Watches content go live and tracks results
Approved content is scheduled and published. The client sees their analytics dashboard — reach, engagement, follower growth — all updating automatically.
06The Takeout Media Side — How the Team Uses It

While the client is going through their journey, here's what the Takeout team sees and does on their dashboard.

What the Takeout team does
1
Sees new signups — or onboards a client themselves
When a client signs up independently, they appear on the Takeout dashboard automatically. But the Account Manager can also create a client account themselves — useful for clients who prefer a white-glove setup or aren't tech-comfortable. Either way, the client ends up in the system.
2
Sets up the brand and connects social accounts
The Account Manager can create the brand profile — name, logo, audience, goals, tone — and connect the client's social media accounts on their behalf. This means a new client can be fully set up and ready to work within the same session as the onboarding call, without waiting on the client to do it.
3
Reviews the brief and communicates with the client
Once the brand is set up, the Account Manager reviews the brief, confirms scope with the client via the platform's messaging, and sets expectations before the content team begins work.
4
The content team starts creating
Designers, copywriters, and strategists are assigned to the brand. They upload content — captions, visuals, videos — and add internal notes. The Account Manager can see everything but the client sees nothing yet.
5
Account Manager reviews and sends to client
When the work is ready, the Account Manager reviews it internally and deliberately pushes it to the client's view. This is a manual step — ensuring quality control before the client ever sees anything.
6
Manages approvals and feedback
If the client approves — great, the content gets scheduled. If they request changes, the feedback lands back with the content team. The Account Manager tracks how many revision rounds are happening.
7
Schedules, publishes, and reports
Once approved, the Account Manager schedules the post. The system publishes it at the right time. Analytics pull in automatically and both sides can see the results.
07How Content Moves Through The System

Every piece of content — a post, a video, a carousel — goes through a defined set of stages. This means everyone always knows where things stand, and nothing can accidentally skip a step.

1DraftContent team uploads
2Internal ReviewTeam checks it internally
3Account MgrReviewAccount Mgr approves internally
4Client ReviewClient sees & responds
5ApprovedClient Admin approves
6ScheduledAccount Mgr sets date
7PublishedSystem publishes it
What the client can see at each stage
  • Stages 1–3: Nothing — client sees no drafts
  • Stage 4: First time client sees the content
  • Stage 5–7: Fully visible in their portal
  • Client can only comment on stages 4 and 5
What happens if content is rejected
  • Client clicks "Request changes" and adds a comment
  • Content goes back to Draft (stage 1)
  • Content team and Account Manager are notified
  • After 3 rejection rounds, system flags it for Account Mgr to review

Account Manager-assisted onboarding: AM creates the client account, fills in the brand brief, and sets everything up on the client's behalf — ideal for white-glove onboarding calls

Two levels: internal (Account Manager approves before client sees) + external (Client Admin approves before publishing)

Three client actions: Approve, Request Changes (with a comment), or Reject

10What If Things Go Wrong?

Real-world situations don't always go smoothly. The platform needs to handle these common hiccups gracefully — without panicking and without losing any work.

⏱
The client goes quiet and doesn't approve content
After 48 hours, the system sends the client a gentle reminder notification. At the same time, the Account Manager is flagged so they can choose to follow up via a direct message through the platform. The content stays exactly where it is — nothing is cancelled or lost. The Account Manager can also reschedule the post's target date if the window is at risk of passing.
✕
The client keeps requesting changes on the same piece of content
Every revision round is logged with the client's comment attached. After three rounds of changes on the same post, the system flags it to the Account Manager so they can have a direct conversation with the client about expectations. The full history of every revision is preserved — nothing is deleted — so the Account Manager can reference the entire back-and-forth.
🔑
The client's Instagram or LinkedIn connection expires
Any posts scheduled for that account are moved to "Held" — they don't get cancelled and no content is lost. Both the Client Admin and Account Manager are notified with a simple re-connect button. Once the client reconnects their account, the Account Manager can release the held posts back to the schedule with one click.
⚠
A post fails to publish at its scheduled time
The system tries automatically three times (15 minutes apart). If all three attempts fail, the Account Manager receives an alert with a plain-language reason (e.g. "LinkedIn rejected the file format" or "Instagram rate limit reached"). The post's approval is still valid — it doesn't need to go back through the approval chain. The Account Manager can fix the issue and re-send with one click.
👤
The Account Manager handling a client leaves the team
An admin can reassign any client account to another team member. The new Account Manager gets a handover summary: which content is pending, any open conversations, any posts scheduled in the near future, and how many approvals are waiting. Nothing falls through the cracks during a transition.
